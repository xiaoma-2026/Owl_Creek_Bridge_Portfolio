01

Introduction / Project Theme

This project focuses on how war is represented not only as public violence but also as private illusion. In Ambrose Bierce's An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge, Peyton Farquhar does not simply face death; he transforms the instant before death into a vivid fantasy of escape, endurance, and reunion. The story therefore asks readers to experience war through the unstable consciousness of a man who wants reality to confirm his romantic self-image.

My central theme is that Bierce links time distortion to Confederate romanticism. Farquhar imagines himself as a heroic civilian-soldier, and his final vision follows that script by turning terror into adventure and home into reward. The visual sequence below traces that transformation from military execution to imagined liberation and then back to the shocking material fact of his death.

Because this assignment also asks for AI-generated images, the site does two things at once. It uses visuals to convey how the reader moves through Farquhar's sensations, and it critiques the limits of those visuals. The images can make emotion immediate, but they can also make illusion look too certain, too coherent, and too heroic.

02

Story Summary

Ambrose Bierce's An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge tells the story of Peyton Farquhar, a Southern planter who is about to be hanged by Union soldiers during the Civil War. The story begins in northern Alabama, where Farquhar stands on a railroad bridge with his hands tied and a rope around his neck. Federal soldiers, a captain, and a sergeant prepare the execution with cold military order. As Farquhar looks down at the water below, his thoughts turn to escape and to his wife and children.

The second section moves backward in time and explains why Farquhar has been captured. He is a wealthy slaveholding planter who supports the Confederacy and dreams of proving himself through heroic action. A gray-clad soldier visits his home and tells him that the Union army is repairing the Owl Creek bridge. The soldier suggests that the bridge could be burned, tempting Farquhar into sabotage. The story then reveals that the soldier was actually a Union scout.

The final section returns to the execution. Farquhar appears to fall from the bridge, break free from the rope, escape through the river, survive gunfire, and run through the forest toward home. His senses become strangely intense, and time seems to stretch far beyond ordinary reality. At last, he sees his wife waiting for him and reaches out to embrace her. In that instant, the fantasy collapses. Farquhar has not escaped. He is dead, and his body swings beneath the Owl Creek Bridge. The story uses this final reversal to show how war, fear, and desire can create a powerful illusion in the last moment before death.

03

Close Reading: Confederate Romanticism and Farquhar's Final Vision

Ambrose Bierce structures An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge through an extreme distortion of narrative time, and that structure reveals Peyton Farquhar's final vision as the last expression of a Confederate romantic self-image. Section I slows time almost to stillness. The soldiers are "motionless," and Farquhar's watch expands a few seconds into a maddening sequence of sounds (Bierce, 1890, pp. 1, 2). This dilation turns execution into ceremony and prepares the reader for a consciousness that can aestheticize its own death.

Section II then moves backward to show that Farquhar's hunger for spectacle is politically and culturally formed. Bierce identifies him as a slaveholding planter and an "original secessionist," locating him within the social order the Confederacy seeks to preserve (Bierce, 1890, p. 3). He longs for "the larger life of the soldier" and "the opportunity for distinction," language that romanticizes war as a stage for masculine honor, adventure, and self-enlargement (Bierce, 1890, p. 3). Excluded from military service, Farquhar compensates by imagining himself as a heroic civilian, "at heart a soldier" (Bierce, 1890, p. 3). His final vision follows that script: danger becomes adventure, suffering becomes glory, and home becomes the ideal reward waiting at the end of a Confederate fantasy (Bierce, 1890, pp. 3, 7).

Section III converts the instant of death into an epic escape. Farquhar's senses become "preternaturally keen": he sees "the veining of each leaf" and hears the smallest movements of water and insects (Bierce, 1890, p. 5). The intensity feels vivid, yet it also exposes illusion, because the scene becomes too perfect, too available to his desire. As the journey continues, alienation deepens. The road is empty, the stars are strange, and language itself turns uncanny in the "whispers in an unknown tongue" (Bierce, 1890, p. 7). Even home appears as an idealized tableau, with his wife waiting like the reward promised by his own script. Bierce's final sentence collapses fantasy into fact and shows that Farquhar dies inside a self-dramatizing ideology.

AI Critique: What the Images Capture and Distort

AI-generated images created with ChatGPT Pro can visualize Peyton Farquhar's final vision as the last expression of a Confederate romantic self-image. In my sequence, I deliberately made Figure 4, "Farquhar swims through gunfire and cannon fire," and Figure 5, "Farquhar imagines returning home to his wife," look cinematic and believable, because Farquhar's fantasy must first feel like a real adventure. The AI captures two key literary devices from Bierce's story: distorted time and heroic self-fashioning. It also reveals a limitation: a single image can present the surface of Farquhar's fantasy more easily than the unstable interior consciousness that produces it.

Bierce begins with a controlled execution scene: Farquhar stands with his hands tied, a rope around his neck, and soldiers arranged in formal silence (Bierce, 1890, pp. 1-2). Then the narration enters his altered perception. The watch sounds slow and painful, and the final instant expands into an imagined escape (Bierce, 1890, p. 2). This fantasy connects to Farquhar's self-image. Bierce describes him as a slaveholding planter, an "original secessionist," and a man who longs for "the larger life of the soldier" (Bierce, 1890, p. 3). The AI images capture that desire by turning helpless execution into heroic action.

Figure 4 places the viewer inside Farquhar's perceived reality. The dark water, splashing bullets, cannon fire, and dramatic composition create panic, bodily struggle, and sensory overload. These details visualize Farquhar's "preternaturally keen" senses, including ripples, insects, leaves, dewdrops, and tiny sounds in the water (Bierce, 1890, pp. 5-6). Figure 5 extends the same fantasy through warm light, the house, the gate, and his wife's waiting body. It visualizes his desire to return home, just before he is "about to clasp her" and the illusion collapses (Bierce, 1890, p. 7). The AI's limitation is that these images cannot fully show, by themselves, the contradiction between heroic self-image and dying hallucination. The final hanging image and written critique turn the adventure back into psychological revelation.

12

Reflection: Time, Sensation, and Farquhar's Imaginary World

The visual texts allow the viewer to experience Farquhar's world by turning Bierce's psychological narration into embodied images. The images work best when they show how Farquhar's mind constructs reality through fear, pain, memory, and desire. His final vision is an imaginary world created in the instant before death.

Time is the most important part of this visual experience. In ordinary reality, Farquhar's execution happens quickly. In his mind, however, one moment expands into a long escape. Bierce shows this when Farquhar hears his watch ticking with unbearable slowness and when his thoughts move rapidly through his brain (Bierce, 1890, p. 2). Figure 3, "The plank tilts, the rope breaks, and Farquhar falls from the bridge," represents this dilation by splitting one instant into several panels. The viewer experiences time as Farquhar does: suspended, stretched, and dreamlike.

The environment also reflects Farquhar's subjective state. The bridge, soldiers, rope, and river create a world of military order and physical pressure (Bierce, 1890, pp. 1-2). Figure 4, "Farquhar swims through gunfire and cannon fire," emphasizes cold water, splashing bullets, darkness, and bodily struggle. These details make the viewer feel panic and sensory overload. When Figure 5, "Farquhar imagines returning home to his wife," shifts to the road home, flowers, golden light, the white gate, and his wife's face, the environment becomes shaped by desire. The viewer sees how Farquhar's longing transforms danger into a fantasy of return.

The images also reveal a defense mechanism: dissociation. Farquhar's mind separates itself from the reality of execution and replaces pain with an escape narrative. Bierce supports this through Farquhar's sharpened senses, such as the ripples, insects, leaves, and dewdrops he notices after falling into the river (Bierce, 1890, pp. 5-6). Figure 6, "The final revelation: Farquhar's body hanging beneath the bridge," forces the viewer to reinterpret everything before it. The visual sequence therefore lets the viewer move through both Farquhar's external death and his internal fantasy.

AI Use Disclosure

For this digital portfolio, I used ChatGPT Pro to generate the six AI images that appear in my visual storytelling sequence. The images were created to illustrate key moments from Ambrose Bierce's An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge, including Farquhar's execution, the flashback at his plantation, the fall from the bridge, the imagined escape through the river, the fantasy of returning home, and the final revelation of his death. I revised my prompts several times to improve the organization of the panels, the emotional expressions, the visual contrast between illusion and reality, and the overall readability of the sequence.

I also used AI assistance during the writing process. I used Youdao Translation AI to polish parts of my English text, especially for grammar, phrasing, and sentence clarity. The main ideas, image selection, story interpretation, and final organization were chosen and reviewed by me. Since this project focuses partly on AI-generated visual storytelling, I also tried to reflect critically on the limits of AI. Some images make Farquhar's fantasy look too real or too heroic, which shows how AI can sometimes simplify literary ambiguity. Therefore, I used the images both as creative visual tools and as objects of critique.
